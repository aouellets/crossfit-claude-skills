# Example 1 — Review from a described class (good demo, weak fault-spotting)

**Prompt:** "Here's how my class went — review me. I taught the power clean, demoed
it from a few angles, broke it into the dip-shrug-pull, and everyone seemed to get
it. During the WOD I mostly counted reps and kept time; I didn't really stop anyone
even though a couple of cleans got ugly when people got tired. Class of 12,
finished on time."

**Context:** Coach ~8 months in; intermediate class.

---

## What was observed (and what couldn't be)
**Source:** the coach's own written description of one class. This lets me judge
explanation, demo, group management, time management, and (by what's described)
observation/correction. I **cannot** judge tone, presence, or exact cue wording
beyond what's reported.

## Strengths with evidence
- **Demonstration — strong.** "Demoed it from a few angles" and broke the lift into
  dip-shrug-pull: multi-angle demo plus a teaching progression is exactly the right
  move for a technical lift.
- **Time management — solid.** "Finished on time" with a class of 12 on a
  technical day is good floor control.
- **Explanation — good.** Sequencing the teach into parts shows clear structure.

## Gaps with evidence
- **Observation & correction — weak (top concern).** "A couple of cleans got ugly
  when people got tired… I didn't really stop anyone." Seeing a fault but not acting
  is the gap — under fatigue, a degrading clean is exactly when intervention matters.
- **Safety — linked to the above.** Letting loaded cleans break down for score is a
  safety call, not just a quality call.
- **Group management during the WOD — thin.** "Mostly counted reps and kept time"
  suggests scorekeeping crowded out active coaching.

## The single highest-priority development area
**Live correction under fatigue.** It outranks everything else because it sits at
the intersection of safety and athlete outcomes: the demo was already good, so the
limiting factor isn't *teaching* the lift — it's *intervening* when it breaks down.
Fixing this protects athletes and improves their lifting more than any other change.

## A concrete practice drill
Pick **one** fault per movement before each WOD and pre-decide your cue and your
stop-threshold. During the WOD, do a "one lap a minute" rule: every ~60 seconds,
scan the room for that one fault and make at least one correction or load/rep scale.
Debrief yourself after: how many degrading reps did you let pass?

| --- | --- | --- |
