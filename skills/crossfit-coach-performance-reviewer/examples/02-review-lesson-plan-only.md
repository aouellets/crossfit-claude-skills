# Example 2 — Review from a lesson plan only (state the limits)

**Prompt:** "Review my coaching. Here's my lesson plan for tomorrow: 0–5 warm-up
general, 5–20 teach the front squat (review, demo, build to working weight), 20–35
WOD (5 rounds: 10 front squats, 200 m run), 35–45 cooldown + mobility. Scaling:
lighter bar for newer folks."

**Context:** Coach asked for a review; no class happened yet.

---

## What was observed (and what couldn't be)
**Source:** a written lesson plan, no delivery. I can review **plan structure,
sequencing, timing, scaling, and safety planning**. I **cannot** review demo
quality, explanation clarity, live observation, correction, cueing, presence,
group management, or engagement — none of that is observable from a plan. Those are
marked **not observable** below, not graded.

## Strengths with evidence
- **Time blocking — clear and realistic.** 5 / 15 / 15 / 10 across 45 min leaves
  real teaching time before the WOD; the technical block (15 min on front squat) is
  appropriately sized.
- **Sequencing — sound.** Review → demo → build to working weight is the right
  teach-up order; squatting before a loaded squat WOD is logical.
- **A scaling plan exists.** You named at least one scale (lighter bar).

## Gaps with evidence
- **Scaling is under-specified (top concern *within the plan*).** "Lighter bar for
  newer folks" doesn't say how the **run** scales (distance? row substitute for
  impact-sensitive athletes?) or what preserves the squat stimulus for someone who
  can't load. A plan should pre-answer the scales you'll actually need.
- **Safety planning is implicit.** No noted stop-conditions or load cap for the
  front squat under fatigue across 5 rounds.
- **Delivery skills — not observable.** Demo, correction, cueing, presence, group
  management, engagement: cannot be judged from a plan. Submit a description or
  observation of the live class to review these.

## The single highest-priority development area
**Make the scaling plan complete and stimulus-specific.** It's the highest-leverage
fix *available in this material*: a plan that pre-specifies every likely scale (load,
the run, an impact alternative) means fewer on-the-fly decisions and a safer, more
inclusive class — and it's the one thing a plan can actually fix before the class
runs.

## A concrete practice drill
For this exact session, write three scaling rows before tomorrow: **Rx /
intermediate / beginner**, each covering *both* the front squat and the run, each
with a one-line note on what stimulus it preserves. Reuse the table format every
time you plan.
