# Example 3 — A strong coach needing a refinement

**Prompt:** "Observation notes on a senior coach, class of 16: warm-up flowed into
the day's movement; demo crisp; named two faults early and cued them ('knees out',
'heels down') and they stuck; scaled three athletes proactively; safety solid, no
red flags; finished 90 seconds over time and a few newer athletes stood around
during the longer rest in the strength block. Where can this coach get better?"

**Context:** Experienced coach; the gym wants a development edge, not a fix.

---

## What was observed (and what couldn't be)
**Source:** structured observation notes of one live class. Good coverage of demo,
fault-ID, correction, cueing, scaling, and safety. **Not visible from the notes:**
exact verbal explanation depth and individual engagement quality beyond the
"newer athletes stood around" note.

## Strengths with evidence
- **Fault-ID + correction — excellent.** "Named two faults early and cued them…
  they stuck": early identification, economical cues, confirmed correction. This is
  the hard part done well.
- **Proactive scaling — strong.** "Scaled three athletes proactively" before they
  failed shows reading the room.
- **Safety — clean.** Explicitly solid, no red flags.
- **Demo — crisp.** Noted directly.

## Gaps with evidence
- **Class-management density in slow blocks (top concern).** "A few newer athletes
  stood around during the longer rest in the strength block" — the coaching was
  strong while *active*, but the dead time in the strength block left newer athletes
  unguided. That's where engagement and learning leak for a senior coach.
- **Time management — minor.** "90 seconds over" is small but worth tightening at
  this level.

## The single highest-priority development area
**Coach the rest, not just the work — engagement during low-density blocks.** For an
already-strong coach this is the refinement with the most upside: the active coaching
is excellent, so the marginal gain is in the *gaps* between sets, especially for
newer athletes who most need attention exactly when nothing is happening.

## A concrete practice drill
In the next strength block, assign yourself a "rotate and touch" loop: during each
rest interval, give one newer athlete a single specific input (a setup check, a
load call, one cue to think about on the next set). Aim for every newer athlete
getting at least one touch per rest. Note who you missed.

## Follow-up rubric (re-score next session, 0–2 each)
| Skill | Last | Target |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Demo | 2 | 2 |
| Fault-ID + correction | 2 | 2 |
| Proactive scaling | 2 | 2 |
| Safety | 2 | 2 |
| Engagement in low-density blocks | 1 | 2 |
| Time management | 1 | 2 |

This is an honest development review; it can't guarantee any formal evaluation
outcome.
