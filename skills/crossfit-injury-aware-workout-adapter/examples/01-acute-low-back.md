# Example 1 — Tweaked low back, acute (conservative, refer if red flags)

**Prompt:** "I tweaked my low back doing deadlifts yesterday. It's sore and stiff
but I can walk fine. What can I train today without making it worse?"

**Context:** Intermediate, no clinician seen yet, full gym. No numbness, tingling,
leg weakness, bowel/bladder changes, or trauma reported.

---

## Stated restriction / clinician limits
Acute low-back strain from deadlifting yesterday; sore and stiff, walking fine. No
clinician involved yet, so no formal limits exist — this adaptation is conservative
by default.

## Red-flag screen
Checked: **no** leg numbness/tingling/weakness, **no** loss of bowel/bladder
control, **no** major trauma, **no** rapidly increasing swelling. If any of those
appear, stop and seek prompt medical evaluation before training. Given the acute
strain, see a clinician if it is not clearly improving in a few days, or sooner if
it worsens. (Per safety-and-referral.)

## Objective to preserve
Keep training stimulus and blood flow without loading or provoking the lumbar spine —
maintain the engine and train unaffected areas while the back settles.

## Movements to AVOID (today)
Loaded spinal flexion and heavy hinging: deadlifts, good mornings, barbell
back/front squats to depth under load, heavy kettlebell swings, GHD sit-ups,
toes-to-bar, heavy carries, and anything that reproduces the pain.

## Allowed patterns
Pain-free, spine-neutral, low-load work: walking/easy bike or row (only if seated
rowing is pain-free), light unilateral upper-body work, supported pressing, and
gentle pain-free movement within comfortable range.

## Range & loading constraints
Stay in **pain-free range only** and light loads today. Do not chase ROM or load;
nothing should increase the back symptoms during or after.

## Substitutions
- Hinge stimulus → omit today; substitute easy aerobic work to keep the engine.
- Squat stimulus → box sit-to-stand in a pain-free range, bodyweight only, if
  comfortable.
- Midline → replace flexion-based core (sit-ups, T2B) with gentle pain-free
  anti-extension holds if tolerated, otherwise skip.

## STOP conditions
Stop immediately for: pain shooting/radiating into the leg, new numbness/tingling/
weakness, the back "giving way", or pain that increases during or after. Any of
these → stop and seek evaluation.

## Questions for the clinician
- Is this a simple muscular strain or is imaging/assessment warranted?
- What loaded hinging/flexion is safe, and from when?
- Any movements I should specifically avoid during recovery?

## Reassessment criteria
Reassess daily. Reintroduce light hinging only once it is pain-free through range in
daily life, progressing load gradually. If it is not improving in a few days,
plateaus, or worsens, see a clinician before progressing.
