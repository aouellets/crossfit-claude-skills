# Example 2 — Scaling Fran for a beginner (keep it fast and short)

**Prompt:** "How should a beginner do Fran? 21-15-9 thrusters (43/30 kg) and
pull-ups."

**Context:** Beginner, thruster feels heavy at 30 kg for reps, 0 strict pull-ups,
no injuries.

---

## Identified stimulus
Short, glycolytic sprint (2–7 min, ideally under ~5–6 for the intended burn). The
point is a **fast, redline couplet** — "done right" feels like an all-out lung/leg
burn with minimal rest, *not* a grind. The descending rep scheme is built for
speed.

## Limiter
**The pull-up (no strict reps)** and **thruster load** at the intended fast pace. If
either forces long stalls, the workout drops out of its short time domain.

## Levers chosen
**Load** (lighten the thruster so it cycles fast) + **skill** (regress the pull-up
to something they can do in quick sets). We keep the **rep scheme and time cap** —
those define the sprint stimulus.

## Scaling paths
- **RX (above):** 43/30 kg thrusters + pull-ups, 21-15-9. *Preserves:* the sprint.
  *Trades:* nothing — for the capable athlete.
- **Intermediate:** thrusters @ 30/20 kg, banded pull-ups that allow ~7+ unbroken.
  *Preserves:* fast cycling and the short domain. *Trades:* absolute load/skill.
- **Beginner:** thrusters @ empty bar or 2×7.5 kg dumbbells, ring rows (feet
  forward) for pull-ups, 21-15-9 unchanged. *Preserves:* the descending sprint that
  finishes in ~4–6 min. *Trades:* the bar-hang pull, but **keeps it fast**.

## Masters note
Keep the load light enough for touch-and-go; protect shoulders by capping pull-up
set size rather than slowing the whole piece.

## Bad-scale warning
Keeping RX weight and giving the beginner **strict, slow band-assisted pull-ups**
turns Fran into a 12-minute grind — that **destroys the glycolytic sprint
stimulus**. "Heavier but slower" is the classic Fran scaling error: it changes the
time domain and the energy system. Better to lighten and keep the clock honest.

## Risks & stop conditions
Stop for dizziness, chest pain, or thruster form collapse under fatigue. A true
redline raises HR sharply — appropriate for the stimulus, but stop if symptomatic.
