# Example 3 — Incomplete description (provisional read, states limits)

**Prompt:** "My deadlift feels off. What am I doing wrong?"

**Context:** No video, no angle, no load, no rep count, no specifics, no mention of
pain or its absence.

---

> **Provisional read.** There is not enough to analyze a specific rep yet. Below is
> what *can* be said and exactly what I'd need to actually diagnose the fault.

## Observations
None I can rely on — "feels off" is a sensation, not an observation of the
movement. I will not invent faults to fill this in.

## What cannot be determined
Without a video/clear description I cannot determine the setup, bar path, back
position, lockout, or timing. I also don't know the load, rep count, your level,
or **whether there is any pain** — and pain would change the response entirely.

## Prioritized faults
*(Cannot prioritize faults from no observation.)* The most common deadlift faults
to look for once you film are: lower-back rounding, hips shooting up first
(turning it into a stiff-leg), bar drifting away from the shins, and an incomplete
lockout. I'm naming these as **things to check**, not faults I've seen.

## Cues
Held until there's something to analyze (general cues would be guessing).

## Drills
Held until the fault is identified — targeting the wrong fault wastes your time.

## What improvement looks like
To be defined against the actual fault once observed.

## Retest
**To unlock the analysis, send:** (1) a **side-on** video of 2–3 reps, (2) the
**load**, and (3) whether you feel **any pain** (vs. just "off"). If there is sharp
or localized pain, stop loading it — that's a referral, not a coaching cue (see
safety-and-referral), and I'd route you to the injury-aware adapter rather than
analyze form.
