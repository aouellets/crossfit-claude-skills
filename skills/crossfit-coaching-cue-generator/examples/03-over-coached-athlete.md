# Example 3 — The over-coached athlete: stop talking, use a constraint

**Prompt:** "I keep cueing this athlete's snatch every single rep and it's getting
worse, not better. What cues should I add?"

**Context:** Intermediate, no pain. The athlete is visibly tense and overthinking;
the coach has already given many cues this session.

---

## Target
Snatch — quality degrading under a flood of cues; the real problem is
**over-coaching**, not a missing cue.

## Working read
The athlete is in "paralysis by analysis." Adding another cue is the wrong move —
the limiter is now cognitive load, not a position fault. The correct prescription
is **fewer** words and a constraint that produces the right movement on its own.

## Prioritized cues
1. **Try first — say almost nothing:** give **one** simple external cue ("fast
   through the middle") and then go silent for the whole set. Let the athlete feel
   reps without commentary.
2. **Backup:** if you must intervene, replace the cue with a constraint (below)
   rather than a new verbal cue.

## Cue menu by type
This is the case where the menu is the wrong tool. Resist stacking verbal cues.
Use at most one external-focus cue, and prefer the constraint/drill instead.

## Constraint or drill
Drop to a tall-snatch or hang-power-snatch from a fixed start position with lighter
load. The constrained start removes the variables the coach was chasing, so the
correct path emerges without verbal cueing. Let the setup do the coaching.

## When to stop cueing
**Now.** The signal to stop is already present: more cues are making it worse. Give
one cue, then let the athlete take 5–8 reps in silence and self-correct. Re-engage
only if a clear, single fault persists across several reps — and then with a
constraint, not another sentence.
