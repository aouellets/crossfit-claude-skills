# Example 1 — Deconditioned 50-year-old beginner ramp (happy path)

**Prompt:** "How should I train a deconditioned 50-year-old who's just starting
CrossFit? No medical issues they've mentioned."

**Context:** Sedentary for years, no diagnosed conditions stated, full equipment.

---

## Population & key considerations
A deconditioned middle-aged beginner: limited current work capacity, low movement
familiarity, slower recovery than a younger trained athlete, and the highest
priority of all — **building a tolerable, repeatable habit without an early injury
or overreach**. See
[../../../references/coaching-principles.md](../../../references/coaching-principles.md)
§7.

## What to adjust
- **Volume:** start low; short sessions; add volume only as recovery proves it.
- **Intensity:** sub-maximal, RPE-guided; intensity is added last (mechanics →
  consistency → intensity).
- **Impact:** minimize early (row/bike over running/box jumps) to protect joints.
- **ROM / skill:** scaled ranges and simple movements first; build positions before
  load.
- **Recovery:** 2–3 sessions/week early; deliberate rest days.

## What to emphasize
Position quality and aerobic base. Win the first 4–6 weeks on consistency and
sound mechanics, not on scores. Light, frequent practice of the foundational
patterns.

## What to avoid or defer
- **No automatic maximal testing** — it is a stressor, not free information.
- Defer high-impact plyometrics, high-volume novel eccentrics, and "blast them on
  day one" sessions.

## Scope limit & required professional guidance
This is coaching-level adaptation, not a medical evaluation. Given the age and a
long sedentary period, **recommend a medical check-up before high-intensity work
is REQUIRED if any cardiovascular risk factors, symptoms, or known conditions
surface**. If the athlete later reports chest pain, unusual breathlessness, or
dizziness, stop and refer for evaluation — do not program around it. See
[../../../references/safety-and-referral.md](../../../references/safety-and-referral.md).

## Reassessment
Reassess at ~4 weeks: is recovery keeping up, are positions holding under light
fatigue? Progress volume/intensity only on that evidence; hold or re-refer if
soreness is disproportionate or any warning sign appears (rhabdo caution for a
deconditioned athlete).
