# Example 2 — Masters (60+) recovery-biased adaptation

**Prompt:** "I coach a 63-year-old who's trained for years and wants to keep
competing in the masters division. How do I adapt their training?"

**Context:** Experienced, healthy, no current injuries, full equipment.

---

## Population & key considerations
An experienced masters (60+) athlete: capable and trainable, but with reduced
recovery capacity, more cumulative joint wear, and a higher cost of overreaching
than a younger athlete. Progression is real — it is just **slower and more
recovery-dependent**. See
[../../../references/coaching-principles.md](../../../references/coaching-principles.md)
§7 and §9.

## What to adjust
- **Volume:** consolidate — quality over sheer volume; fewer junk reps.
- **Intensity:** keep hard days hard but reduce their frequency; more clear
  hard/moderate/recovery separation across the week.
- **Impact:** manage repeated high-impact exposure (box jumps → step-downs when
  fatigued; cap consecutive high-impact days).
- **ROM / skill:** maintain mobility deliberately; keep high-skill work but warm in
  thoroughly.
- **Recovery:** the lever that matters most — protect sleep, spacing, and deloads.

## What to emphasize
Strength maintenance, mobility, and joint care; aerobic base. Smart competition
prep that doesn't trade long-term joints for a short-term score.

## What to avoid or defer
- Avoid stacking maximal/high-impact sessions back-to-back.
- Defer ego-driven maximal attempts when fatigue or mobility is compromised; no
  automatic max testing.

## Scope limit & required professional guidance
Coaching-level adaptation only. **Any new joint pain that changes movement,
cardiovascular symptoms, or a known condition REQUIRES clinician input** before
continuing to load it — route to `crossfit-injury-aware-workout-adapter` or a
qualified professional rather than coaching through it. See
[../../../references/safety-and-referral.md](../../../references/safety-and-referral.md).

## Reassessment
Reassess recovery and joint status every microcycle. If cumulative fatigue rises
or a niggle appears, pull volume/impact before it becomes an injury; progress only
when recovery is clearly keeping pace.
