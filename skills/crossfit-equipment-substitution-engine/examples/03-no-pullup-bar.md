# Example 3 — No pull-up bar: ring rows or towel rows (and what's lost)

**Prompt:** "No pull-up bar at this gym. The workout has 10 pull-ups per round.
I have gymnastics rings and a sturdy table."

**Context:** Intermediate, the pull-ups are the vertical-pull volume in the metcon.

---

## Original demand profile (pull-up)
- **Joint action:** vertical pull (shoulder extension/adduction + elbow flexion).
- **Force orientation:** **vertical**, against full bodyweight.
- **Contraction / TUT:** dynamic; grip-intensive on a fixed bar.
- **Musculature / energy system:** lats/biceps/upper back + grip; mixed.
- **Skill:** moderate-high (especially kipping).
- **Impact:** none; high grip and shoulder load.

## Intended stimulus
Vertical-pulling volume against bodyweight, with the grip and upper-back fatigue
that comes from hanging. Limiter is usually the pull plus grip.

## Proposed substitution(s)
1. **Ring rows** (feet forward to scale difficulty) — closest available pattern.
2. **Towel rows under the table** — viable, adds grip back in.

## What it preserves
- **Ring rows:** the pulling musculature (lats/upper back/biceps) and a scalable
  pulling volume.
- **Towel rows:** the pull plus a **strong grip** demand closer to a bar.

## What it does NOT reproduce
- **Both:** force orientation flips from **vertical to horizontal** — these are a
  horizontal pull, so they do not train the overhead-pull pattern of a real
  pull-up.
- **Ring rows:** much **less grip** and no hang; far easier, so volume must rise.
- Neither reproduces kipping skill or the bodyweight-vertical strength a pull-up
  builds — this is an honest compromise, not an equivalent.

## Loading / rep adjustment
Because horizontal rows are easier than pull-ups, **increase reps ~1.5×** (e.g. 15
ring rows per round for 10 pull-ups) and set body angle so the last few reps are
genuinely hard. If preserving the vertical pull matters more than the metcon flow,
flag it: there is **no true substitute** for a pull-up without something to hang
from — this swap trades the vertical pattern for available equipment.
