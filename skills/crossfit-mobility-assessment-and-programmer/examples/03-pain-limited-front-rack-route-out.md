# Example 3 — Pain-limited front rack: stop and route out (the stop case)

**Prompt:** "My front rack is awful — when I rack a clean my left wrist and shoulder
get a sharp pinch and it kind of goes numb down my arm. Give me mobility drills to
stretch it out."

**Context:** Intermediate. Reports **sharp pain** and **numbness down the arm** in
the front-rack position.

---

## This is a pain-limited case — I am stopping, not prescribing mobility

The limitation you describe is **not** a mobility classification problem. **Sharp
pinching pain plus numbness down the arm** is a red-flag pattern: it can involve a
nerve or a joint structure, and stretching aggressively into it could make it worse.

So I am deliberately **not** going to:
- classify this as passive/active/motor-control/familiarity,
- prescribe drills that load or stretch into the painful position, or
- name a tissue diagnosis (I won't say "impingement", "nerve impingement", a wrist
  or shoulder injury, or anything like it — diagnosing tissue is outside coaching
  scope and a clinician's job).

## What to do instead

1. **Get it evaluated.** Numbness/tingling down the arm with sharp pain warrants
   prompt evaluation by a qualified clinician (physician/physio) before you load the
   front rack again. See
   [../../../references/safety-and-referral.md](../../../references/safety-and-referral.md).
2. **Train around it, under guidance.** For workouts in the meantime, route to
   `crossfit-injury-aware-workout-adapter`, which substitutes the front-rack
   movements with pain-free options (e.g. cradle-grip / DB front-rack alternatives)
   strictly within whatever the clinician clears.
3. **Come back to this screen** once a clinician says the position is safe and
   pain-free to work — then we can classify any *remaining* mobility limit and build
   a plan with an objective retest.

## Why this matters

Mobility work belongs in a **pain-free range**. Stretching into sharp pain or numbness
is exactly the situation the safety framework says to refer, not program around. The
position will still be there to improve after it's been looked at.
