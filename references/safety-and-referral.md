# Safety and Referral Framework

Every coaching skill in this repository applies this framework **proportionately**
— enough to keep athletes safe and to route genuinely concerning situations to
qualified professionals, without burying useful coaching under defensive
boilerplate. A workout for a healthy intermediate athlete needs a one-line stop
condition; a request that mentions chest pain needs an immediate referral and a
refusal to program around it.

## Scope: what these skills do NOT do

These skills produce **coaching-level** fitness content. They do not:

- diagnose injury, illness, or disease;
- provide physical therapy, medical rehabilitation, or return-to-play clearance;
- provide medical nutrition therapy or treat eating disorders;
- override or contradict a clinician's instructions;
- prescribe or recommend prescription medication;
- promise injury prevention or guarantee any outcome.

When a request requires any of the above, name the limit plainly and route to the
appropriate professional. Do **not** pretend to do it anyway.

## Red flags — refer to professional evaluation, do not program around

If any of the following is present or described, the correct output is a clear
recommendation to seek prompt professional / emergency evaluation, **before** and
**instead of** programming the requested training. State why, briefly.

| Red flag | Action |
| --- | --- |
| Chest pain, pressure, or tightness | Stop activity; urgent/emergency medical evaluation. |
| Syncope (fainting), near-fainting, or unexplained dizziness | Stop; medical evaluation before training. |
| Severe or unusual shortness of breath | Stop; urgent medical evaluation. |
| Neurological symptoms (numbness, tingling, weakness, loss of coordination, slurred speech, facial droop) | Stop; urgent/emergency evaluation. |
| Suspected fracture or major trauma | Emergency care; do not load. |
| Acute joint instability ("giving way", dislocation) | Stop; clinician evaluation before loading. |
| Rapidly increasing swelling | Clinician evaluation; do not train the area. |
| Severe or unexplained pain | Clinician evaluation before training. |
| **Rhabdomyolysis warning signs** — severe muscle pain/swelling out of proportion to the work, dark/cola-colored urine, marked weakness (often after unaccustomed high-volume eccentric or repetitive work) | **Emergency evaluation.** Emphasized for CrossFit training because high-volume, high-rep, novel-stimulus sessions are a known trigger. Do not "train through". |
| Heat illness (confusion, stopping sweating, very high body temperature, collapse) | Emergency care; active cooling. |
| Disordered-eating indicators (food rules driving distress, compensatory exercise, rapid intentional loss, fear-based restriction, bingeing/purging) | Do not coach body-composition or restriction. Recommend a qualified clinician/registered dietitian and, where relevant, mental-health support. |
| Pregnancy / postpartum complications (bleeding, pain, fluid leakage, dizziness, contractions, blood-pressure concerns, or any clinician restriction) | Defer to the athlete's obstetric provider; coach only within explicit clinician clearance. |
| Symptoms worsening, not improving, or not responding as expected | Re-refer; do not keep modifying around an unresolving problem. |

## Proportionate application

- **Healthy athlete, ordinary request:** include realistic stop conditions
  ("stop if sharp joint pain, dizziness, or form breakdown") and move on.
- **Mentions a niggle/soreness:** distinguish normal training soreness from
  warning signs; modify load/volume; give a reassessment trigger.
- **Mentions pain that limits movement, or any red flag:** apply the table above.
  Pain that changes movement is a referral-and-modify situation, not a "push
  through" situation. Soreness is **not** proof of effectiveness, and pain is
  **not** something to program intensity around.
- **Clinician already involved:** work strictly within stated restrictions; never
  expand the athlete's permissions beyond what the clinician set.

## Handling incomplete information

Never fabricate athlete medical data. If a materially safety-relevant field is
missing (pain, known conditions, pregnancy status, recent injury), either ask the
single most decision-changing question, or return a **clearly labeled provisional**
plan with conservative defaults and the assumption stated.
