# Coaching Principles (specialized for CrossFit)

Domain-general training principles, applied to mixed-modal CrossFit / general
physical preparedness (GPP). These are the assumptions every programming,
scaling, and coaching skill should reason from. They are not generic platitudes —
each is stated as a usable decision rule.

## 1. Train for broad, general, inclusive fitness (GPP)

CrossFit optimizes **work capacity across broad time and modal domains** — not a
single sport quality. Programming should, over a complete cycle, develop the ten
recognized general physical skills: cardiorespiratory endurance, stamina,
strength, flexibility, power, speed, coordination, agility, balance, accuracy.
A plan that only improves one quality at the cost of others is, by this
definition, worse general fitness even if a number went up.

## 2. Mechanics → consistency → intensity

Earn the right to add intensity. Sound positions first, then repeatable
consistency under fatigue, then load/speed. Intensity is **relative** to the
athlete and is the variable most associated with results **and** with risk —
so it is added last and scaled per person, never imposed by the clock or the
whiteboard. Intensity is not a substitute for programming logic.

## 3. Stimulus is the unit of programming

Every workout has an **intended stimulus** — the adaptation and the experience it
should produce (e.g. "10–12 min sustained aerobic grind, never redlining" vs.
"sub-4 min all-out sprint"). Loads, reps, time caps, and movements are means to
that end. Programming, scaling, and substitution all answer to the stimulus, not
to the literal prescription. See [workout-design-framework.md](workout-design-framework.md)
and [stimulus-preserving-scaling.md](stimulus-preserving-scaling.md).

## 4. Intensity is relative and must be distributed

Not every session can or should be maximal. A coherent week distributes
intensity (e.g. hard / moderate / skill-or-recovery), manages repeated joint
stress and impact, and places recovery deliberately. Constantly-maximal
programming is not "more CrossFit" — it is a recovery and injury problem. See
[time-domain-framework.md](time-domain-framework.md).

## 5. Progressive overload still applies

Mixed-modal training does not exempt strength and skill work from structured
progression. Olympic lifts, gymnastics skills, and absolute strength respond to
planned, measurable progression with retests — not to random daily variation.
"Constantly varied" governs the **conditioning stimulus mix**, not whether your
back squat has a plan.

## 6. Specificity for goals; generality for GPP

The default is broad GPP. When an athlete has a defined goal (a competition, a
movement, a lift), bias the plan toward that goal's demands while controlling
interference with the rest of fitness. See
[competition-strategy.md](competition-strategy.md).

## 7. Individualization

Age, training age, history, injuries, recovery capacity, schedule, equipment, and
goals all change the right answer. Always individualize from intake data; when
data is missing, label assumptions and ask only what changes the output. See
[athlete-intake.md](athlete-intake.md).

## 8. Measure and retest

Programming is a hypothesis. Build in benchmarks and **retests** so changes are
visible and decisions are evidence-based rather than vibes-based. Avoid automatic
maximal testing (it is a stressor, not free information) and never treat soreness
as a substitute for a measured result.

## 9. Recovery is part of the program

Sleep, nutrition, and planned deloads are inputs, not afterthoughts. A plan the
athlete cannot recover from is not a good plan, regardless of its content.

## 10. Coach honestly

State assumptions, surface tradeoffs, and never manufacture certainty, standards,
or athlete data. A clearly labeled provisional answer beats a confident fabricated
one. Safety always takes precedence — see [safety-and-referral.md](safety-and-referral.md).
