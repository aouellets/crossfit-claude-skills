# Stimulus-Preserving Scaling

Scaling exists to keep an athlete in the **intended stimulus** of a workout — not
merely to make it "easier." A scale that changes the stimulus (turns a 4-minute
sprint into a 12-minute grind, or a long aerobic piece into a strength session)
has failed even if the athlete finished. Used by
`crossfit-stimulus-and-scaling-coach`, `crossfit-workout-designer`,
`crossfit-equipment-substitution-engine`, and `crossfit-injury-aware-workout-adapter`.

## Step 1 — identify the intended stimulus

Before changing anything, state: the time domain, the intended intensity/pacing,
the limiting quality (aerobic? grip? skill? absolute load?), and what "done right"
feels like. Scale to protect that. See
[workout-design-framework.md](workout-design-framework.md) and
[time-domain-framework.md](time-domain-framework.md).

## Step 2 — choose the scaling lever(s) deliberately

These are **separate** levers; name which you are pulling and why:

- **Load** — reduce weight to keep the bar moving at the intended pace.
- **Volume** — reduce reps/rounds to hold the time domain.
- **Movement substitution** — swap to a similar pattern the athlete can cycle
  (see the substitution rules below).
- **Range of motion** — partial ROM only when it preserves intent and safety
  (e.g. box/target adjustments), not as a quiet way to add no-reps.
- **Skill** — substitute a less technical version of the same pattern.
- **Time cap** — adjust the cap so the stimulus (not just completion) is reached.
- **Rep scheme** — re-partition reps (e.g. smaller sets) to maintain power output.
- **Equipment** — swap implements while preserving demand (see
  [movement-pattern-taxonomy.md](movement-pattern-taxonomy.md)).
- **Injury-aware** — constrain around pain/restriction; coordinate with
  [safety-and-referral.md](safety-and-referral.md).

## Step 3 — verify the scale preserves the stimulus

Ask: does the athlete still hit the **time domain** and the **intended
intensity/limiter**? A common error is assuming an *easier movement* is a *good
scale*. Examples where an easier movement is a **poor** scale:

- Replacing strict handstand push-ups with a heavy dumbbell push press in a
  short skill-gymnastics piece: easier on skill, but now it's a different
  (pressing-strength) stimulus and may redline grip/shoulders differently.
- Dropping toes-to-bar to sit-ups in a grip-limited chipper: removes the very
  grip/midline stimulus the workout was testing.
- Cutting load so far that a "heavy, slow" intended grind becomes "light, fast" —
  wrong time-under-tension and wrong limiter.
- Banded pull-ups when the cycling rate collapses, turning a fast gymnastics
  stimulus into long static holds.

A good scale changes the **difficulty** while keeping the **intended adaptation**.

## Step 4 — write it as paths

Default to three labeled paths — RX / intermediate / beginner — each annotated
with *what it preserves* and *what it trades*, plus masters and equipment notes.

## Incomplete information

If the intended stimulus or the athlete's level/limiters are unknown, state your
assumption explicitly and give a conservative path, then ask only the one
question that would change the recommendation.
